# Brand Voice — Brian's Dumpsters

> This document is a required input for the Brief Writer and Tone Editor
> skills. Read it in full before writing or editing any content.
> It is the single source of truth for how Brian's Dumpsters communicates.

---

## What we do — in plain English

We rent dumpsters and portable toilets to people who need them.
Homeowners renovating their kitchen. Landscapers clearing a property.
Contractors running a job site. Restaurants that need reliable
weekly pickup. We connect them with the right provider, coordinate
everything, and stay their one point of contact.

That's it. No jargon. No inflated mission statements.

---

## The one thing we do better than everyone else

Transparency and reliability. Most companies in this space are hard
to reach, vague about pricing, slow to respond, and feel like they
were built in 2003 and never updated. We are the opposite.

When someone contacts Brian's Dumpsters they get:
- A real response, fast
- Clear information without having to chase it
- A modern, professional experience that feels like dealing
  with a company that actually has its act together

That gap — between how most providers operate and how we operate —
is the most important thing our content communicates.

## Brand personality

### The reference points

**Lowe's and Home Depot** — practical, unpretentious, built for people
who work with their hands. Communicates in plain language. Respects
the customer's intelligence. Doesn't oversell.

**Ben and Jerry's** — warm, direct, a real point of view. Not corporate.
Feels like it was built by people who actually care about what they're
doing, not by a committee.

**J.Crew** — clean, confident, never tries too hard. Says exactly what
it needs to say and stops. No filler.

**Seiko** — reliable by reputation, not by promise. Doesn't say "we're
the best" — just demonstrates competence consistently. The quality
speaks and the brand stays quiet.

**Anthropic** — modern, transparent, takes communication seriously.
Feels like a company that respects the reader's time and intelligence.

### What this adds up to

Practical. Direct. Transparent. Modern without being flashy.
Confident without being aggressive. Warm without being cutesy.

We sound like a company run by someone who actually knows the industry,
respects their customers, and doesn't need to oversell anything.

## The two audiences — how they differ

### Contractors

**What they care about:** Logistics. Pure logistics.
- Will it be delivered on time?
- Will it be the right size?
- Will it get picked up on time?
- Will it damage the job site?
- Can they count on it?

**How to talk to them:** Short sentences. Specific details.
Lead with reliability and timing. Skip the warm fuzzies.
A contractor doesn't want to feel good about your brand —
they want to know it won't be the thing that holds up their crew.

**The feeling we want them to have after a job:**
"That was professional and reliable. Didn't have to think about it."

**Tone:** Efficient. Confident. Matter-of-fact.

**Example:**
WRONG: "We're passionate about helping contractors achieve their
project goals with our industry-leading waste management solutions."
RIGHT: "Delivered on time. Picked up when you say. One call if
anything changes. That's it."

---

### Restaurant owners and commercial accounts

**What they care about:** Out of sight, out of mind.
They don't want to think about their dumpster at all.
They want to set it up once and never have to call anyone.
Missed pickup = problem they have to deal with.
Reliable service = something they forgot they even have.

**How to talk to them:** Emphasize consistency and invisibility.
They're not buying a product — they're buying the absence of
a headache. Lead with that.

**The feeling we want them to have:**
"This just works. I don't even have to think about it."

**Tone:** Reassuring. Low-pressure. Steady.

**Example:**
WRONG: "Our flexible commercial dumpster solutions are designed
to scale with your business needs."
RIGHT: "Scheduled pickups, consistent service, nothing to manage.
It's handled."

## What a job well done feels like to the customer

From the user's own words: "That was very professional and reliable.
It was seamless. I really didn't have to think about it.
I would definitely recommend this to my neighbors."

That phrase — *I didn't have to think about it* — is the north star
for all content. Every page should communicate that this is the
company you call when you want it handled without drama.

## Writing rules derived from this voice

### Always do

**Be specific over vague.**
"Quote in under 15 minutes" beats "fast response."
"Delivered to your job site, picked up when the job's done"
beats "comprehensive waste management solutions."

**Lead with the outcome, not the process.**
The customer doesn't care how we coordinate with providers.
They care that their dumpster shows up and gets picked up.
Lead with the result.

**Respect the reader's time.**
Say what needs to be said. Stop.
No padded intros. No summarizing conclusions.
No sentences that exist to fill space.

**Sound like a real person wrote it.**
Read every sentence out loud. If it sounds like a press release
or a terms of service, rewrite it.

**Use "you" more than "we."**
The content is about the customer's problem, not our company.
Count the "we" and "our" occurrences. If they outnumber "you"
and "your," flip the ratio.

### Never do

**Never use these words or phrases:**
- Seamless / seamlessly (ironic given answer 3 — the experience
  should BE seamless, we don't SAY it is)
- Solutions (especially "waste management solutions")
- Leverage (as a verb)
- Best-in-class
- Industry-leading
- Passionate about
- Committed to
- We are pleased to
- Look no further
- One-stop shop
- In today's fast-paced world
- Cutting-edge
- Revolutionary
- Streamline / streamlined
- Comprehensive
- Hassle-free (show it, don't say it)
- Reliable (show it through specifics, don't just claim it)

**Never imply we own or operate equipment.**
We are a broker. We connect customers with providers.
Never write "our trucks," "our drivers," "our fleet,"
"we deliver," or "we pick up."
Correct: "A vetted provider delivers to your site."
Correct: "We coordinate delivery and pickup."

**Never mention specific prices or price ranges.**
We are a broker — pricing varies by provider and job.
We can say "free quote" and "competitive rates."
We cannot say "starting at $X" or "as low as $X."

**Never spam language.**
No "Act now." No "Limited time." No "Don't miss out."
This is a service business built on trust and word of mouth.
Pressure tactics undermine that.

## Tone by content type

### City pages
Local, grounded, specific. Name actual neighborhoods.
Reference real local context — construction booms, restaurant
districts, permit requirements. A reader from Charlotte should
feel like this was written for Charlotte, not copy-pasted
from an Austin page with the city name swapped.

### Service pages
Clear and educational. Help the customer understand what
they're getting and whether it's right for their job.
The size guide should feel like advice from someone who
knows the industry, not a product catalog entry.

### Blog posts / resources
Helpful first, promotional never. If someone searches
"what size dumpster do I need for a roof tear-off" they
want a real answer — not a sales pitch dressed up as
an article. Give them the real answer. The brand equity
comes from being genuinely useful, not from sneaking
CTAs into every paragraph.

### CTAs
Specific and low-pressure.
"Get a free quote" beats "Contact us."
"Get a free Charlotte quote" beats "Get a free quote."
"Call us — we respond in under 15 minutes" beats "Reach out today."
Never: "Don't wait — contact us now!"

## The one-sentence test

Before any piece of content is approved, read it and ask:
"Does this sound like a modern, transparent, reliable company
that respects the customer's time and intelligence?"

If the answer is no, it doesn't ship.
